# The air travel experience: a research synthesis for next-generation product design

**The air travel industry sits at an extraordinary inflection point where $45 billion in unrealized retail value, 87.87% booking abandonment rates, and a widening gap between traveler expectations and delivered experiences create a once-in-a-generation opportunity to build a transformative travel product.** Airlines are paradoxically the only industry to improve on Forrester's CX Index in 2024, yet sentiment analysis of 15,000+ traveler reviews reveals satisfaction has fallen *below pandemic lows*. This tension—between incremental digital progress and fundamental experience failure—defines the white space. The convergence of agentic AI, biometric identity, Starlink-era connectivity, and shifting traveler demographics (Gen Z now spends $11,766 annually on travel, more than any generation) means the next great travel product will not optimize the existing journey but redefine it entirely.

---

## 1. A decade of paradox: how air travel CX evolved from 2015 to 2026

The past decade in air travel CX follows a clear arc: **unbundling fragmented the experience, COVID accelerated digital adoption, and the post-pandemic recovery exposed structural gaps that digital tools alone cannot fix.**

**The pre-pandemic era (2015–2019)** was defined by relentless unbundling. Airlines stripped base fares and charged separately for bags, seats, and meals while basic economy proliferated across U.S. network carriers. By 2016, only 34% of global air bookings by value were through airline-direct online channels. PwC's 2017 survey revealed a **negative 33% gap** between customer expectations and actual experiences—the largest of any industry surveyed. Booking abandonment reached **87.87%** for airlines specifically, compared to the cross-sector average of 76.9%.

**The pandemic (2020–2021)** collapsed airline revenue by 60% but paradoxically improved satisfaction. Forrester's CX Index peaked at 72 in 2021 and IATA's Global Passenger Survey jumped to 82% satisfaction—likely because lower volumes let airlines deliver better service. More critically, COVID forced rapid adoption of contactless processes, mobile check-in, and digital health credentials that became the foundation of post-pandemic expectations.

**The recovery era (2022–2024)** brought "revenge travel" demand—2024 airline gross bookings reached **115% of 2019 totals**—but infrastructure didn't keep up. European departure delays hit their highest since 2003. Flight irregularities grew from 20% to **36% of all TripAdvisor reviews**. Deloitte found 62% of aviation respondents' workforces were half their pre-pandemic size or smaller. The critical shift: airline-direct online bookings rose from 34% to **49% of global bookings by value**, signaling travelers' growing willingness to engage airlines directly.

**The current state (2025–2026)** shows divergent trajectories. J.D. Power's 2025 study found overall satisfaction up 6 points, driven by economy improvements—but premium economy satisfaction fell 7 points and first/business fell 1 point. IATA's 2025 GPS shows 50% of travelers now use biometrics (up from 30% in 2022), and **78% want a single smartphone app combining digital wallet, passport, and loyalty cards**. Forrester found airlines were the *only* industry to improve CX quality in 2024, while U.S. CX overall hit an all-time low.

### Six macro forces reshaping the travel experience

The forces converging on air travel are structural, not cyclical. **AI and agentic technology** represent the most transformative: McKinsey projects agentic AI could "upend the travel industry," yet fewer than 25% of travelers frequently use AI tools when booking and only 2% trust AI with full booking autonomy. **Biometric identity** has reached a tipping point with 50% airport usage, 86% satisfaction, and 73–75% of passengers preferring biometrics over traditional ID—though 58% cite data breach concerns. **The unbundling-to-rebundling cycle** presents a **$45 billion opportunity**: McKinsey's conjoint analysis of 7,000 travelers reveals airlines' rigid fare bundles fail to capture full willingness to pay, with 46% of travelers preferring bundled purchases and 70% of 18–24-year-olds using bundles often or always. **Gen Z travelers** spend the most per capita on travel, are mobile-native (90% prefer digital wallets), and trust influencer recommendations (45%)—they will define expectations. **Post-COVID behavioral shifts** have made bleisure bookings surge 275% year-over-year, with two-thirds of corporate travelers extending trips for leisure. Finally, **sustainability pressure** is mounting: 79% of Gen Z travelers prefer accommodations with sustainable practices, though airline CEO commitments show "softening" near-term.

## 2. The quantified cost of poor experience—and the business case for transformation

The financial case for transformative CX investment is unambiguous. **Each U.S. airline leaves up to $1.4 billion in annual revenue unrealized** by failing to optimize customer experience, according to Forrester. The costs of poor UX manifest across every metric.

Booking abandonment is the most visible hemorrhage. Airlines face an **87.87% abandonment rate**—11 points worse than the cross-sector average—with mobile abandonment reaching 85.27%. Baymard Institute's research identifies the primary drivers: 48% abandon because extra costs (taxes, fees) were too high, 24% because forced account creation was required, and 22% because checkout was too long or complicated. Critically, 32% of mobile travel bookings are abandoned due to slow loading alone. IATA found that **19% of passengers did not purchase ancillaries because of payment friction**—direct revenue leakage that compounds across millions of transactions.

CX quality drives shareholder value with measurable precision. Watermark Consulting's 16-year study found **CX leaders generated 5.4 times the shareholder return of CX laggards**, outperforming the S&P 500 by approximately 110 points cumulatively. Bain & Company's research shows a 1-point NPS increase correlates with a **1.3% revenue increase** for airlines. J.D. Power's 2025 data makes the loyalty economics stark: 81% of passengers rating their experience "perfect" say they "definitely will" fly that airline again—this drops to just **4%** among those rating the experience "poor."

Forrester's analysis of customer-obsessed firms (only 3% qualify) reveals they report **41% faster revenue growth**, 49% faster profit growth, and 51% better customer retention. The operational cost of disruptions alone reaches **$60 billion annually** globally, with delays costing an additional $30 billion in direct costs. These are not incremental optimization opportunities—they represent fundamental gaps between current delivery and achievable performance.

## 3. Best-in-class digital products: what the leaders do differently

### The leading airline apps and their distinguishing design choices

**Qatar Airways** won "Best Airline App 2025" at the World Aviation Festival for its balance of design simplicity and powerful functionality—biometric logins, multi-passenger boarding pass management, AI-powered chat assistance, and deep Privilege Club integration that serves both frequent flyers and first-time travelers equally. **Emirates** took "World's Best Airline App 2024" at the World Travel Tech Awards, with robust digital-physical integration across the entire journey. **Delta's Fly Delta app** (4.7–4.8 stars) leads among U.S. carriers with its "Today" view consolidating all day-of-travel information on one screen, an always-available offline boarding pass, and the forthcoming Delta Concierge AI assistant for proactive wayfinding, packing alerts, and visa requirement notifications. **Singapore Airlines** excels through ecosystem thinking: the SingaporeAir app handles booking-to-boarding, while Kris+ enables local merchant miles earning and Pelago powers tours and activities—all connected through KrisFlyer. **Southwest** consistently scores highest on UX benchmarks (92nd percentile SUPR-Q in MeasuringU studies) through radical simplicity: transparent pricing, no hidden fees, and uncluttered booking flows.

Among non-airline apps, **Hopper** defines mobile-first travel with 120 million+ downloads, AI price prediction claiming 95% accuracy, a color-coded calendar (green = cheap, red = expensive), "Watch This Trip" push notifications, and fintech-style products like Price Freeze and Cancel for Any Reason. Its Gen Z/Millennial user base (70% of users) validates that the next generation of travelers expects fundamentally different digital experiences.

### Booking flow UX: what Baymard and NNG research reveals

Baymard Institute's benchmark of 10 airline sites across **240+ UX parameters** yielded a sobering conclusion: overall travel booking UX performance is "poor to mediocre," with only one site delivering a "decent" experience and none achieving "good" or better. The most critical failures include homepages that offer strong branding but weak directional support for the primary booking function, missing industry-specific filters (40% of travel sites lack them), and forced account creation that drives 24% of abandonment.

Nielsen Norman Group's omnichannel framework identifies five components that separate best-in-class from mediocre airline digital experiences: **consistent** (cohesive across all channels), **optimized** (each channel designed for its specific device context), **seamless** (effortless channel transitions), **orchestrated** (proactively leading customers through journeys), and **collaborative** (enabling simultaneous multi-channel use). NNG cites airlines as a best-practice industry, noting they "successfully lead me through the entire travel process, from booking to boarding"—but this applies only to the best performers.

The data reveals a critical platform bifurcation: **desktop is used primarily for research and booking** (61% look at airfares, 48% purchase tickets), while **mobile dominates day-of-travel tasks** (52% check-in, 35% flight status, 34% boarding passes). Users explicitly feel more confident booking on desktop. This means mobile should optimize relentlessly for day-of-travel experiences while desktop should optimize for research, comparison, and purchase flows.

### Personalization, loyalty integration, and multi-modal planning

Best-in-class personalization moves beyond recommendation engines to **anticipatory intelligence**. Delta Concierge combines "context of who customers are and how they like to travel" with airline operational knowledge to deliver proactive passport expiration alerts, weather-based packing suggestions, and TSA PreCheck Touchless ID notifications. United MileagePlus uses AI to analyze individual travel patterns and deliver tailored flight and hotel offers with dynamic mile valuations. Hopper's personalized price predictions with push notifications have created compelling, game-like engagement patterns that drive repeat usage.

Loyalty program digital integration represents enormous economic stakes: Delta SkyMiles is valued at approximately **$28 billion**, United MileagePlus at $22 billion, and American AAdvantage at $24 billion. The best implementations embed loyalty earning and redemption into every screen (Delta), build connected ecosystems of apps and services (Singapore Airlines with KrisFlyer/Kris+/Pelago), and use gamification elements like mileage challenges and seat upgrade games that drive **10x engagement increases**. Delta's free Wi-Fi for all SkyMiles members creates a powerful data-capture loop: connectivity enrollment drives membership, membership drives data collection, and data collection drives personalization.

For multi-modal journey planning, **Delta leads with its Uber integration** (SkyMiles earning on rides, in-app pickup instructions at Delta hubs) and Joby Aviation partnership for future air taxi connectivity. Singapore Airlines' ecosystem approach—Kris+ for local transport and dining, Pelago for activities—earns miles across every journey segment. However, **no airline has achieved fully seamless end-to-end trip integration**; most still hand off to partner apps for ground transport, hotels, and activities. This represents the single largest product opportunity in travel.

## 4. The airport experience: where digital meets physical

### Biometrics have reached the tipping point

**98% of airlines** have implemented or are planning biometric boarding. Delta's TSA PreCheck Touchless ID—the industry's first domestic biometric program—delivers bag drop transactions **75% faster** (30 seconds vs. 2 minutes) and security verification **60% faster** than standard TSA PreCheck (7–10 seconds vs. 20–25 seconds). Star Alliance's biometric system (NEC I:Delight platform) covers security, lounge access, and boarding at Frankfurt, Munich, Vienna, and Hamburg, with passengers registering once via app and using face recognition at all participating touchpoints. Singapore Changi plans 95% immigration automation by 2026 with **10-second clearance times** through its One Changi ID biometric single-token system.

IATA's One ID initiative—based on W3C Verifiable Credentials and ICAO Digital Travel Credentials—completed its first proof-of-concept in October 2024, demonstrating fully digital air travel on a Hong Kong-Tokyo roundtrip. The EU's Entry/Exit System goes live October 2025, requiring non-EU travelers to register facial and fingerprint data. The trajectory is clear: **biometric-enabled, document-free travel is moving from aspiration to implementation within 2–3 years**.

### How the world's best airports integrate digital and physical

**Singapore Changi** (Skytrax World's Best Airport 13 times, IDC Future Enterprise of the Year 2024) has invested S$3 billion+ over six years in smart airport technology. Its DIVA Digital Factory with Accenture develops AI and IoT products, while its Living Lab Programme tests solutions with government agencies and ground handlers. Changi is now exploring **agentic AI that auto-triggers cascading actions during disruptions**—activating cleaners, managing service recovery, making announcements, and optimizing resources without human intervention.

**Amsterdam Schiphol** epitomizes the "Bytes not Bricks" philosophy—using digital capabilities to solve problems that can no longer be solved physically. Its Dynamic Time Slots system lets passengers pre-book security check times, achieving **99.6% of passengers waiting less than 10 minutes** and steering 7.5% of traffic to off-peak moments. Its Flow Balancing digital twin prevented 4,000+ minutes of crowding in 2024. The Passenger Experience Platform (Travor) won Skytrax Best Digital Services 2023, boosting CSAT by 18% while handling 90%+ of inquiries autonomously. Critically, Schiphol shares airport data via open APIs so airline apps (KLM, Delta, United) can integrate Schiphol data directly—a model for airport-airline digital collaboration.

**Incheon International Airport** became the first and only airport to achieve ACI Customer Experience Accreditation Level 5 (highest tier) and is pioneering **agentic AI video analytics** with Superb AI and NVIDIA for real-time passenger flow management using natural language queries. Its AI-Star autonomous guide robots provide wayfinding, take photos with passengers, and send them via SMS—turning functional touchpoints into emotional connections.

**Hamad International Airport** deploys CT-based security screening (Smiths Detection HI-SCAN 6040 CTiX) that eliminates the need to remove laptops from bags, processes transfer passengers with 95% queuing under 5 minutes, and surrounds its ORCHARD garden with a 1.2km ultra-high-resolution LED ribbon containing 169 million+ pixels.

### Real-time disruption management and the $60 billion IROPS problem

Irregular operations cost airlines **$60 billion annually**, with U.S. impact alone reaching $30–34 billion. The best disruption management has shifted from reactive to proactive. **Delta leads** with automatic rebooking when flights are canceled (same options via app, website, and kiosks as reservation specialists), multi-channel notification within 30 minutes, and proactive SkyMiles deposits before complaints are filed. During Winter Storm Hernando (February 2025), Delta proactively messaged customers with fare-difference waivers and encouraged early rebooking. **American Airlines' AI-powered flight-hold system** at DFW and CLT uses AI to predict which outbound flights can be briefly delayed to wait for connecting passengers, saving **200,000+ travelers and thousands of missed connections** during severe weather. **KLM's BCG-built Sentry and Runway tools** for day-of-operations disruption management achieved a **20–30% reduction in non-performance costs**.

## 5. In-flight and post-flight: the connected cabin and the last-mile gap

### Starlink is fundamentally reshaping in-flight connectivity and IFE strategy

Starlink has disrupted inflight connectivity using Low-Earth Orbit satellites delivering **latency under 99ms and speeds up to 500 Mbps**—7x faster than conventional satellite WiFi. United Airlines leads with **300+ aircraft equipped** and 7 million+ passengers served across 129,000+ flights, with plans for 800+ aircraft by end of 2026 and free WiFi for all MileagePlus members. Qatar Airways' Starlink-equipped 777s achieve download speeds exceeding 200 Mbps. British Airways' parent IAG signed a deal covering 500 of 600 jets. Hawaiian Airlines, airBaltic, Air France, Alaska Airlines, and Korean Air are all in active rollout.

This connectivity revolution is shifting the IFE paradigm toward **hybrid models** where seatback screens deliver curated content while high-speed WiFi enables personal streaming subscriptions. The IFE hardware race has escalated to cinema-grade displays: **Thales FlytEDGE** delivers 4K QLED HDR screens with a 96TB onboard data center for edge caching, while **Panasonic Avionics Astrova** offers 4K OLED screens with 67W USB-C fast charging and access to personal subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, Apple Music). The IFE market is projected to nearly double from **$6.44 billion in 2024 to $13.21 billion by 2033**.

Cathay Pacific tops the 2025 Skytrax IFE rankings with its "My Journey" interface letting passengers personalize their environment—scheduling work, eat, watch, and sleep modes. Emirates ICE maintains the world's largest media library with **6,500+ channels in 50 languages**. Emirates has also deployed **20,000 Apple devices to cabin crew** (AED 32 million+ investment) with custom apps showing passenger profiles, Skywards status, dining preferences from prior flights, and real-time seat change data—enabling truly personalized service at scale.

### The post-flight last-mile remains the biggest integration gap

Ground transportation integration represents the most tangible near-term opportunity. **15% of all Uber trips** begin or end at an airport, yet most airline apps still redirect to partner platforms rather than embedding booking. Delta's Uber partnership (SkyMiles earning on rides, step-by-step in-app instructions from plane to pickup) and United's in-app display of Uber availability, wait times, and pricing represent current best practice—but true in-app ground transport booking remains nascent.

Baggage tracking has improved dramatically—mishandling rates are **down 67% over 20 years** despite doubled passenger volumes—but cost the industry $2.6 billion in 2022 from 26 million mishandled bags. SITA's WorldTracer now covers 500+ airlines and 2,800 airports with Auto Reflight returning 60% of mishandled bags in 1.6 days. A major innovation: SITA integrated **Google Find Hub** so passengers with personal trackers can share encrypted bag location directly with airlines, transforming consumer trackers from passive reassurance into active recovery tools. **87% of passengers** say they'd be happier checking bags with real-time airline updates.

Hotel, car rental, and destination content integration within airline apps remains thin. Airlines increasingly position as "experience orchestrators" but most still redirect to partner websites. TripIt (SAP Concur) and AirAsia MOVE represent the closest approaches to true multi-service integration, while Delta Concierge's destination-aware AI assistance (visa alerts, weather-based packing, wayfinding) points toward the future.

## 6. AI in air travel: from hype to measurable impact

### What is working today beyond chatbots

AI's most meaningful airline applications are in **operations optimization and disruption management**, not customer-facing chatbots. Alaska Airlines' Flyways AI (with Air Space Intelligence) presents optimization opportunities for **55% of flights**, delivering 3–5% fuel savings on flights over 4 hours and saving **1.2 million gallons of fuel annually** (11,958 metric tons CO₂). KLM's BCG-built AI suite for schedule optimization and day-of-operations management reduced non-performance costs by 20–30% and has been deployed at multiple carriers including Cathay Pacific and GOL Airlines. Google and American Airlines' AI-based contrail avoidance experiment demonstrated a **54% reduction in contrail formation**, potentially mitigating 35% of aviation's climate impact.

For customer service, Malaysia Airlines' "Mavis" (launched February 2026) represents the shift from chatbots to true AI agents—autonomously handling travel queries and booking tasks across web, app, and email by acting on real airline systems. Azul Airlines saw **84% reduction in first-contact abandonment** and 59% of new conversations resolved by AI bot within 10 months. McKinsey's own SkyLink conversational booking tool reduced corporate travel booking time from **10 minutes to under 2 minutes** with 30,000+ bookings processed.

### Dynamic pricing AI is mature but politically radioactive

Delta announced AI would set prices on approximately **20% of domestic flights by year-end 2025** using technology developed with startup Fetcherr, reporting "amazingly favorable unit revenues" from tests. Accelya's FLX ONE processes **31+ billion offers daily** using AI for real-time pricing. However, Delta's announcement triggered immediate Congressional backlash with formal Senate inquiries, Harvard and Northeastern experts raised alarms about "surveillance pricing," and CU Boulder research warned that short-term revenue gains could erode long-term trust. The distinction between traditional dynamic pricing (adjusting by demand and timing) and individual-level personalized pricing (using consumer data) remains the critical ethical and regulatory line.

### Agentic AI: enormous industry enthusiasm, minimal consumer readiness

The agentic AI travel ecosystem is forming rapidly. **Sabre, PayPal, and MindTrip** are launching the first end-to-end agentic booking pipeline in Q2 2026—natural language to Sabre Mosaic APIs (420+ airlines, 2 million hotels) to PayPal payment, all within a conversation. Google AI Mode is working with Booking.com, Expedia, Marriott, and others on agentic booking with 75 million daily active users already. Perplexity Comet, ChatGPT, and Google Project Mariner can all complete flight bookings through browser automation.

But consumer trust lags dramatically behind industry enthusiasm. **Only 2% of consumers** are willing to give AI full autonomy for travel bookings, and only 25% are "comfortable" letting AI agents plan travel. Skift's March 2026 analysis was bluntly titled: "Travel Brands Are Building AI Agents for a Consumer That Doesn't Exist." McKinsey identifies four scenarios for agentic AI adoption—Copilot Commerce, AI Experience Curation, Direct-Booking Renaissance, and Agent Takeover—and positions the industry in the Copilot/Curation phase through 2028. BCG estimates airlines achieving AI-first positioning can sustain a **5–6% higher margin advantage**, but 90% of the industry is still experimenting without P&L impact.

### The credible AI timeline

- **Now–2027**: Contact center copilots, AI-assisted disruption rebooking, price prediction tools, route optimization, crew digital tools with AI-powered passenger insights
- **2027–2029**: Conversational natural-language booking at scale, proactive disruption prediction hours in advance, hyper-personalized ancillary offers, cross-supplier automated rebooking
- **2029–2033**: AI-to-AI negotiation between customer and supplier agents, fully autonomous booking for trusted travelers, predictive schedule adjustment days in advance, AI-optimized contrail avoidance at scale
- **2033+**: Single-token biometric journeys with no document presentation, autonomous aircraft turnaround, AI-designed aircraft and cabin configurations

## 7. The two travelers: premium/business and leisure/family deep dives

### Premium/business travelers value time above all—and the experience is getting worse for them

Global business travel spending is projected at **$1.57 trillion in 2025** (6.6% year-over-year growth). Premium cabins contribute **nearly half of all passenger revenue** while representing roughly 15% of total seats—making this segment disproportionately valuable. Yet J.D. Power 2025 shows premium economy satisfaction down 7 points and first/business down 1 point, even as economy improves.

Business travelers' top values are **schedule flexibility** (70% cite it as top-three), **productivity ROI** (49% of travel buyers call it a strategic priority), and **face-to-face connection** (83% call business travel enjoyable; top benefits are networking at 51% and exploring cities at 47%). Their top pain points are **general fatigue** (55%), **flight cancellation/rescheduling friction** (the #1 in-trip pain point per Deloitte), and **rising costs** (74% of travel managers cite budgets not keeping pace). A telling quote from a GBTA 2025 buyer: "If it takes more than 90 seconds to book a room, our travelers will go elsewhere—even if it costs more."

The corporate travel platform landscape is being disrupted. SAP Concur dominates enterprise but receives a "C" grade for ease of setup with 3–6-month implementation timelines and dated UX. **Navan** has emerged as the challenger with AI-powered unified travel-expense-card management, employee rewards for cost-effective bookings, and average booking time under 6 minutes. Critically, **65% of business travel spending remains unmanaged**, and 30% of buyers are reevaluating their TMC in 2025—39% citing technology dissatisfaction.

Premium pre-departure experiences set expectations that digital products must match. Emirates offers chauffeur service at most destinations (Mercedes-Benz S 450 for First Class). Lufthansa's Frankfurt First Class Terminal provides a personal assistant, expedited security, restaurant, and dedicated car to the aircraft. Qatar Airways' Al Safwa Lounge, Singapore Airlines' The Private Room, and Air France's La Première Lounge create experiences that feel like stepping outside the airport entirely. The digital product opportunity: **replicate this sense of effortless, personalized orchestration in the app experience**.

### Leisure/family travelers face compounding friction that no product has solved

**92% of parents** are likely to travel with children in the next 12 months, spending an average of **$8,052 on family travel in 2024** (up 20% year-over-year). Yet 73% cite affordability as the #1 challenge, 63% of Americans find traveling with kids stressful, and 83% of traveling parents have felt judged for their children's behavior.

The specific pain points for families are both emotional and logistical. **Seated-together policies** create anxiety: 45% of passengers find it unacceptable to be charged for seat selection, and airlines vary widely in family seating guarantees (United offers free seating for 1 adult + 2 kids under 12; Southwest provides family boarding between Groups A and B). **Booking form tedium** compounds with each passenger: American Airlines requires full contact details and residence for each traveler, prompting frustrated parents to ask "Why do I need to fill the residence for each person flying if they are my kids?" **13% of traveling families have children with special needs**, and they gave the industry a **C- on inclusivity**, with 50% reporting challenges in safety, staff training, and accessibility.

For travel inspiration and discovery, the gap between what exists and what families need is significant. Parents primarily use digital tools to find deals (55%) and manage budgets (47%), but only 19% have used a travel advisor—while **61% would consider one** for exclusive benefits and peace of mind. This suggests a product opportunity for AI-powered advisory that combines deal-finding with the trust and personalization of a human advisor. The "kidfluence" trend is real: 74% of parents with children over 7 say their kids love to travel, and children aged 7–18 are active "co-pilots" in planning, finding inspiration via social media and digital platforms.

Bundling UX represents both opportunity and risk. Priceline shows packages saving up to $625 versus booking individually, and real-time savings displays create compelling purchase momentum. However, SmarterTravel testing found that in some cases booking components separately was actually cheaper than the bundle—and cancellation policies vary by component within a package (hotel may be flexible while flight is not), creating hidden complexity that erodes trust.

## 8. Service design orchestration: maintaining context when everything goes wrong

### True omnichannel remains rare despite being the #1 CX driver

Wunderman Thompson research found **42% of consumers say seamless cross-channel experience is a "top expectation"**—yet only 11% of airline decision-makers see it as the most important factor. This perception gap explains why most airlines deliver multichannel (present on many channels) rather than true omnichannel (connected, context-preserving across channels). Companies with genuine omnichannel approaches see engagement rates up to **250% higher**, 89% first-time-buyer-to-loyal-customer conversion, and 14.6% annual revenue increase.

LATAM Airlines represents a benchmark: after overhauling CX using Zendesk Suite and Sunshine Conversations, they made WhatsApp their primary channel (one of WhatsApp's top-using brands globally), enabling proactive flight change updates, coordinated bot-to-agent handoffs, and in-chat rebooking, meal selection, and upgrades. Delta's multi-channel context system automatically rebooks passengers across app, website, and kiosk while notifying via push, email, and SMS—and free onboard Wi-Fi lets passengers rebook mid-flight when about to miss connections.

NNG's service blueprint framework identifies the critical "information reuse" principle: data gathered early in the journey must be repurposed later backstage, creating three effects—customers feel recognized, employee effort is reduced, and data stays consistent. The biggest blocker to this is **siloed systems**: as one industry analysis noted, "many larger carriers take a siloed approach—three different systems sending notifications about three different things—that's not a unified experience."

### Anticipatory design is the next frontier

The shift from reactive to anticipatory service design represents the highest-leverage opportunity. **71% of travelers expect personalized and proactive service**, and 76% become frustrated when this expectation is not met. Airlines with CX as their foremost priority see **12x return on sales**. Current best implementations include Delta's proactive pre-storm messaging with fare-difference waivers, British Airways' automated delay messaging for delays exceeding 30 minutes with self-service links, and Changi Airport's exploration of agentic AI that auto-triggers cascading disruption responses.

The anticipatory design framework requires four layers: zero-party and first-party data collection, ML-based pattern recognition identifying leading indicators of dissatisfaction, automated proactive interventions triggered before problems manifest, and continuous feedback loops. The technology exists today through platforms like Cirium Proactive Services (used by Expedia, Kayak, BCD Travel) and AI sentiment analysis tools—the gap is organizational, not technological.

## 9. Accessibility: regulatory pressure accelerating inclusive design

Accessibility in air travel has entered a period of unprecedented regulatory and financial consequence. **American Airlines was fined $50 million in October 2024**—the largest airline disability penalty in history—for unsafe physical assistance that resulted in injuries to wheelchair users. The DOT's December 2024 Final Rule creates a **rebuttable presumption of violation** when checked wheelchairs are returned damaged, mandates 24-hour delivery of delayed wheelchairs domestically, and requires annual hands-on training for all wheelchair-handling employees. The European Accessibility Act, enforced from June 2025, applies WCAG 2.1/2.2 Level AA standards to airline websites and apps with penalties up to €250,000 per violation in France.

Digital accessibility remains inconsistent. An industry audit found Virgin Atlantic scored 8/10 on WCAG technical criteria but only **1/10 on consumer journey usability**—demonstrating that compliance does not equal accessibility. Blind screen reader users document severe issues with custom-styled dropdown menus unreadable by assistive technology, disappearing keyboard focus, and inability to complete check-in. United Airlines leads in investment, redesigning its app for VoiceOver and TalkBack compatibility and adding Braille to aircraft cabin interiors.

AI is opening new frontiers: **GoodMaps Explore** at Portland International provides AI-powered indoor navigation for blind, deaf, and neurodiverse travelers. Aira offers free AI visual assistance for blind travelers at Denver International. VR experiences help passengers with autism familiarize themselves with airports pre-travel. Thales is developing signing avatars in multiple languages using 70–80% AI-generated sign language. These innovations serve the **27 million passengers with disabilities** who traveled by air in 2019—but also improve the experience for everyone through clearer wayfinding, simpler interfaces, and more transparent information architecture.

## 10. Metrics that matter: measuring what best-in-class looks like

The measurement ecosystem for airline CX has matured significantly but contains critical gaps.

**NPS benchmarks** show wide variance: JetBlue leads U.S. carriers at **50**, followed by Southwest (48), Delta (43), Alaska (40), American (32), and United (25). The 25-point gap between highest and lowest underscores how CX strategy—not just product investment—drives perception. Globally, the airline industry average is approximately 33.

**J.D. Power's 2025 rankings** reveal that **ease of travel and trust are the #1 and #2 satisfaction drivers**—more important than value for price paid. Across segments: JetBlue leads first/business (738), Delta leads premium economy for the third consecutive year (717), and Southwest leads economy for the fourth consecutive year (694). The study's most actionable finding: fewer than 10% of passengers experienced problems, but those who did rated satisfaction dramatically lower—meaning **reliability, not luxury, is the foundation of satisfaction**.

**Customer Effort Score (CES) is the most underutilized metric in aviation.** Only 25% of companies use CES despite it being the metric most correlated with purchase frequency, cross-selling, and upselling. Gartner's research shows 96% of customers experiencing high-effort interactions become less loyal, while only 9% with low-effort experiences report disloyalty. Most airlines measure NPS and CSAT but not CES at individual touchpoints—a measurement gap that means airlines don't systematically track effort at the Moments of Pain and Moments of Truth that define the journey.

**Skytrax's 2025 World Airline Awards** rank Qatar Airways #1 (9th time), followed by Singapore Airlines, Cathay Pacific, Emirates, and ANA. No U.S. carrier holds Skytrax 5-star status; Delta ranks highest among U.S. airlines at 21st globally. The awards' methodology—covering in-flight products, cabin service, and ground service across 100+ nationalities—provides the most comprehensive global passenger assessment, though methodological transparency has been questioned.

## 11. White spaces and unmet needs: where the transformative product lives

The research reveals ten distinct white spaces that define the opportunity for a next-generation air travel product.

**The fragmented journey** is the #1 unmet need. Travelers navigate 5–10+ separate platforms for flights, hotels, ground transport, activities, insurance, payments, visa requirements, and connectivity. **97% of travelers** want a system giving access to many travel-related services in one place, and 78% want one app combining digital wallet, passport, and loyalty cards. Nielsen found the average traveler spends **53 days visiting 28 websites over 76 sessions** to plan and book a trip. No single player owns the complete door-to-door experience.

**The disruption recovery gap** represents the second-largest opportunity. When things go wrong, passengers are largely abandoned: long hold times, no proactive alternative routing across airlines, manual rebooking across providers. Only 4% of "poor experience" passengers will definitely rebook, making recovery design existentially important for retention.

**The mid-journey engagement void** is striking. Between booking and flying (often days or weeks), airlines have almost no touchpoint. Post-flight engagement is virtually nonexistent. This void represents lost opportunity for destination preparation, anticipation-building, and ancillary revenue.

**The economy wellness gap** persists: airlines invest heavily in premium cabins while economy—the majority of passengers—sees diminishing comfort. Premium cabins generate ~50% of revenue from ~15% of seats, leaving the majority underserved in jet lag mitigation, air quality, seat comfort, and wellness features.

**The accessibility-by-design gap** means 12% of passengers requiring special assistance rate it below "excellent," hidden disabilities (autism, neurodiversity, anxiety) remain underserved, and digital tools rarely accommodate diverse needs despite clear regulatory momentum.

### What the transformative next-generation experience looks like

The research points toward a product built on three foundational capabilities:

**AI-orchestrated journey intelligence** where agentic AI manages complexity on the traveler's behalf—handling discovery through planning through booking through payment in one conversational experience, detecting constraints between suppliers, optimizing combinations humans wouldn't surface, and handling cross-provider rebooking as one coherent action. The supply side stays fragmented, but the traveler experience becomes unified.

**A biometric identity layer** where single enrollment replaces all ID checks from home to destination—integrating passport, visa, health records, loyalty, and payment into one verifiable digital credential that the traveler controls.

**A proactive disruption shield** where AI predicts delays and cancellations before they happen, automatically rebooks across airlines, adjusts hotel and ground transport, communicates via preferred channel, and turns the industry's biggest failure point into its strongest trust-building moment.

The product that achieves these three capabilities—while serving both the premium traveler's need for **effortless efficiency** and the family traveler's need for **confident simplicity**—will not compete with existing airline apps. It will make them feel like the antiquated systems they are built on.

## Conclusion: the synthesis

Five insights emerge from this research that should guide product design decisions:

**Reliability beats luxury.** J.D. Power's finding that ease and trust matter more than price, combined with the dominance of flight irregularities in negative reviews (36% of all feedback), means the foundational product value must be operational confidence—knowing what's happening, what will happen next, and what to do when things change.

**The $45 billion rebundling opportunity requires radical personalization.** McKinsey's conjoint data proves travelers will pay for what they value—but static fare bundles capture only a fraction of willingness to pay. Price represents only 34% of booking decision weight; baggage allowance (16%), seat selection (10%), and ticket flexibility (9%) collectively matter almost as much. AI-powered dynamic bundling that matches individual preferences represents the largest revenue opportunity in the industry.

**Consumer trust, not technological capability, is the binding constraint on AI adoption.** With only 2% of consumers trusting full AI autonomy for bookings, the product must be designed for graduated trust-building: AI as copilot first, then curator, then autonomous agent—earning trust through demonstrated competence at each stage.

**The product must serve the full journey or lose the relationship.** Airlines' direct booking share grew from 34% to 49% in eight years, proving travelers want direct relationships—but only if the product delivers value beyond the flight itself. The average traveler's 53-day, 28-website planning odyssey is a failure of product design, not traveler behavior.

**Accessibility is not compliance—it is a design philosophy that improves every experience.** Features designed for travelers with disabilities (text-based directions, clear typography, simple navigation, proactive communication) benefit every traveler, including those in noisy airports, those traveling with children, and those navigating unfamiliar languages. The $50 million American Airlines fine signals that the regulatory environment will increasingly penalize airlines that treat accessibility as an afterthought.
